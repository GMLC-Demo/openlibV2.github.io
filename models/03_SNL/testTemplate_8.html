Brief Theoretical Background:

Distributed generation (DG), including residential photovoltaic (PV), is generation in small units connected at various locations in the distribution and subtransmission systems at voltage levels below that typically represented in detail in system planning studies. The preferred way of modeling known (or estimated amounts of DG is to specify the P and Q values of the distributed generation (DG) as separate entries in the power flow “load” data, including the following:

  • Pdg – MW output of distributed generation
  • Qdg – MVAr of distributed generation (sign convention same as generators)
  • Stdg – DG status (0 – offline; 1 – online)

A new WECC Composite Load model, named CMPLDWG, is used to model both load and DG for dynamic analysis. The DG model is added at the load bus while the load modeling will be identical to that in the earlier composite load model, CMPLDW. This simplified model for inverter-based generation (e.g. residential PV), shown in the figure to the right, will be used in the CMPLDWG. The input parameters required for the model are given in the “WECC Composite Load Model with DG Specification” (hyperlink below).

Model Specifications:



Assumptions:

  • Developed with a focus on usage in commercially available positive sequence stability software programs
  • Models are suitable for studying system response to electrical disturbances, not solar irradiance transients
  • Simulations performed using these models typically cover a 20-30 second time frame, with integration steps in the range of 1 to 10 milliseconds
  • Appropriate limits for the converter power and current must be modeled



Limitations:

This model is not intended:
  • For use in performing studies of phenomena outside of the typical range of frequencies of interest in power system stability studies (i.e. 0.1 to 3 Hz)
  • For detailed analysis of unbalanced system phenomena
  • For detailed local studies associated with control tuning and design for the interconnection of central PV plants to very weak systems (i.e. short-circuit ratios below 2 or 3)

Model Dependencies:

This model is not a stand-alone model and depends on a complete power system model such as those developed for the large interconnection systems in North America.

Interfacing Information:

The CMPLDWG model is available in Siemens PTI PSS®E, GE PSLF™, and PowerWorld Simulator. Specifications on input parameters and output variables for the substation transformer, shunt capacitor, distribution feeder equivalence, and load component models are detailed in the “WECC Composite Load Model Specification.” The input parameters and output variables for the DG model are detailed in “WECC Composite Load Model with DG Specification.”

Model Validation:

Through numerous validation cases it has been shown that this model adequately captures the dynamic time-domain behavior.

Metrics for validation:

A complete description of the guidelines for model validation can be found in the WECC “Generating Unit Model Validation Policy” available on the WECC website.

The measure of success of the validation a demonstration of reasonable agreement between the recorded and simulated results. Important characteristics used to determine reasonable agreement include the following:

  • general shape of the measured and simulated curves, including magnitude and rate of the response
  • rise time, overshoot and bandwidth
  • dead-bands and delays
  • initial and final values
