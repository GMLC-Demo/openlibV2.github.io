Brief Theoretical Background:

Similar to Type 4 wind turbine generators and photovoltaic plants, a Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) connects to the grid through an inverter. Thus, the primary aspect of this model is the power inverter interface between the storage mechanism (battery) and the grid. This same model could potentially be applied for modeling other storage technologies, so long as the interface between the storage device and the grid is a power electronic, voltage source converter.

The power converter in BESS allow for full four quadrant control of the active and reactive current output independently within the current rating limits of the power electronics. This type of power converter in normally referred to as a voltage source converter.

Dynamic representation of large-scale BESS for system planning studies requires the use of two or three modules developed for wind and PV under the auspices of the WECC. Those modules are:

  • Plant controller module
  • Generator/converter module
  • Electrical controls module

Model Specifications:

  • Developed with a focus on usage in commercially available positive sequence stability software programs
  • Models are suitable for studying system response to electrical disturbances, not solar irradiance transients
  • Simulations performed using these models typically cover a 20-30 second time frame, with integration steps in the range of 1 to 10 milliseconds
  • Appropriate limits for the converter power and current must be modeled

Limitations:

This model is not intended:
  • For use in performing studies of phenomena outside of the typical range of frequencies of interest in power system stability studies (i.e. 0.1 to 3 Hz)
  • For detailed analysis of unbalanced system phenomena
  • For detailed local studies associated with control tuning and design for the interconnection of central PV plants to very weak systems (i.e. short-circuit ratios below 2 or 3)

Model Dependencies:

This model is not a stand-alone model and depends on a complete power system model such as those developed for the large interconnection systems in North America. For example, shunt capacitors and any other reactive support equipment required for the central station PV power plant must be modeled separately with existing standard models.

Model Validation:

At present there is no significant field test data available for validation. BESS modeling is an active research area and it continues to evolve with changes in technology and interconnection requirements, so BESS model validation against reference data remains a challenge due to limited industry experience. Metrics for validation:

A complete description of the guidelines for model validation can be found in the WECC “Generating Unit Model Validation Policy” available on the WECC website.

The measure of success of the validation a demonstration of reasonable agreement between the recorded and simulated results. Important characteristics used to determine reasonable agreement include the following:

  • general shape of the measured and simulated curves, including magnitude and rate of the response
  • rise time, overshoot and bandwidth
  • dead-bands and delays
  • initial and final values
